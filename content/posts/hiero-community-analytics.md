The [hiero-hackers/analytics](https://github.com/hiero-hackers/analytics) repository exists to make the health of the Hiero ecosystem legible — not just to maintainers, but to anyone curious about where the project is headed. Today we're sharing two charts generated by that tooling, along with the thinking behind them.

---

![Stacked area chart showing open issues by difficulty label (Good First Issue, Beginner, Intermediate, Advanced) from May 2025 to May 2026.](/images/difficulty_over_time_event_based_weekly.png)
*Open Issues by Difficulty Over Time (Event-Based) — weekly resolution*

This chart tracks the number of open issues at any given point in time, broken down by the difficulty label applied to each issue. The four tiers — **Good First Issue**, **Beginner**, **Intermediate**, and **Advanced** — map directly to the labeling convention used across Hiero repositories.

A few things stand out:

**The labeling programme began in earnest in late 2025.** The chart uses an event-based reconstruction that anchors each issue's entry into the time series at the moment its difficulty label was applied, not when the issue was created. This matters because difficulty labels were applied retroactively starting in December 2025, so naïve historical reconstruction would produce a misleading spike. What you see instead is a clean forward-only view: issues enter the dataset when labeled, and exit when closed.

**All tiers grew sharply from January 2026 onward.** By May 2026, the total backlog of labeled open issues sits at around 140 — with Intermediate and Advanced issues making up the majority. This reflects both increased community issue-filing activity and deliberate effort by maintainers to label existing work.

**Good First Issues have grown steadily but proportionally.** The teal band representing Good First Issues now sits at roughly 35–40 issues, a meaningful pool for new contributors looking for an entry point. Growth here has tracked overall growth in the labeled backlog, suggesting maintainers are keeping pace with onboarding-friendly labeling.

### Why event-based reconstruction?

The standard approach to building a "how many issues were open at time T" chart is to replay state changes from GitHub's event stream. The complication for Hiero is that labeling began months after many issues were created. An issue opened in June 2025 and labeled "Intermediate" in December 2025 shouldn't appear in the chart for June — we didn't know its difficulty then. The event-based model solves this by treating the `labeled` event timestamp as the canonical entry point, giving us a dataset that only asserts what was known at the time.

![Stacked bar chart showing unique active contributors by role (General User, Triage, Committer, Maintainer) for 2025 and 2026.](/images/maintainer_pipeline_yearly.png)
*Maintainer Pipeline: Unique Active Contributors by Role — PR & Issue Activity (Yearly)*

This chart counts the number of unique contributors who were active in a given calendar year, segmented by their role in the Hiero GitHub organization.

**Total active contributors more than doubled year over year** — from 282 in 2025 to 596 in 2026. That's a 111% increase in one year.

**General Users drove most of the growth.** The gray segment (General User) grew from roughly 140 to over 400, indicating a large influx of community members filing issues, commenting, and opening pull requests without a formal role in the organization. This is healthy: a wide base of general contributors is the precondition for identifying future Triagers and Committers.

**The upper tiers held steady in proportion.** Triage contributors remain a thin but important band — these are the contributors doing the unglamorous work of labeling, reproducing bugs, and routing issues to the right people. Committers and Maintainers each grew in absolute terms, even if they represent a smaller share of the now-larger total.

The maintainer pipeline framing reflects a deliberate philosophy: open-source sustainability depends on growing contributors through successive levels of trust and responsibility. Watching these numbers over time tells us whether the funnel is working.

## About the analytics tooling

Both charts were generated by scripts in [hiero-hackers/analytics](https://github.com/hiero-hackers/analytics), a community-maintained repository for tracking the health of the Hiero ecosystem. The tooling pulls data from the GitHub API, reconstructs time-series state from raw event streams, and renders charts via Python (matplotlib / seaborn).

If you're interested in contributing — whether to improve the analytics pipeline, extend the visualizations, or help label issues — the repository is open and welcoming. Check the README for setup instructions, and look for issues tagged `good-first-issue` to find a starting point.
